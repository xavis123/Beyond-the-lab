Beyond the Lab Enterprise Robot Adoption and Research Collaboration in Europe

How to use this visualisation

This is my look at how much European businesses actually use robots between 2018 and 2022, and whether that lines up with how strongly each country works together on robotics research.

Guided story: Just scroll down. The sections on the left walk you through the story, and the panel on the right swaps to the matching chart or map as you go. The Key takeaways section also has a year slider that animates the hub-strength versus adoption bars across 2018, 2020 and 2022.

Interact: Hover over anything (a country, bar, dot or link) to see the numbers behind it, and click a country on the map or a network node to pick it out.

Minimap (small corner map): A few steps show a small map in the corner of the chart. Click a country on it and the chart redraws for just that country. Click a different country to switch to it, and click the same country again to go back to the all-EU view (the average across every country). To make the minimap bigger, hover over it. To shrink it again, click the white × in its top-right corner, or just move your pointer off the map and back onto the text.

Explore mode: The last section lets you dig around on your own. You can drag the year slider, switch the map metric (adoption, change, industrial robots, service robots, hub strength, or network only), drag the “minimum joint articles” slider to hide weaker collaboration links, choose which countries appear in the network, hide or show the network, and zoom or drag the map. “Reset all” puts everything back.

Who it’s for: European technology policy analysts, innovation researchers and business readers who are curious about robotics adoption patterns.

Data sources and methodology

Eurostat: Enterprise robot adoption data from the Eurostat ICT usage in enterprises survey (isoc_eb_p3d and isoc_eb_p3dn2). Values represent the percentage of enterprises (with 10 or more employees) using industrial or service robots. Available for 2018, 2020, and 2022.

ETO (Emerging Technology Observatory): Cross-border robotics research collaboration data measuring joint research articles between country pairs. Filtered to complete observations for 2018 to 2022.

Map boundaries: Natural Earth 110m administrative boundaries via world-atlas (public domain).

Terms: “Hub strength” is a country’s weighted number of joint research articles with the other subset countries (its weighted degree in the collaboration network). “pp” means percentage points, the plain arithmetic difference between two percentages.

Note: France 2022 carries Eurostat flag b (break in series) and is retained with caution. The Q3 overlap subset includes countries that appear in both the Eurostat and ETO datasets and that have at least one collaboration link to another subset country (12 countries).

The state of enterprise robotics in Europe

Between 2018 and 2022, Eurostat surveyed enterprises across Europe on their use of industrial and service robots. Meanwhile, the Emerging Technology Observatory tracked cross-border robotics research collaboration.

This visualisation brings these two perspectives together to explore three questions:

  1. How has enterprise robot use changed over time across countries?
  2. What patterns of enterprise robot use can be observed across enterprise sizes and sectors over time?
  3. Which European countries act as hubs in cross-border robotics research, and how does hub status relate to their adoption of enterprise robots?

This visualisation focuses on observed patterns in the available data rather than causal explanation.

Adoption changed unevenly across countries

The chart shows how enterprise robot adoption (% of enterprises with 10+ employees using any robot) changed between 2018 and 2022 for countries with data in both years.

Some countries saw notable increases while others declined or remained stable. The map colours countries by the same change values.

The paired country sample shows mixed movement between 2018 and 2022 rather than a uniform Europe-wide increase. This does not explain why countries changed, but it identifies where the changes were concentrated.

Industrial robots lead, but service robots are present

Breaking adoption into industrial and service robots reveals that industrial robots dominate in most countries. The connecting line shows the within-country gap.

In most valid country observations, industrial robot adoption is higher than service robot adoption. The chart compares composition within countries, but it does not show whether the composition changed over time.

Larger enterprises adopt robots more

Enterprise size is a strong structural factor. Large enterprises (250+ employees) consistently show higher robot adoption rates than small (10-49) and medium (50-249) enterprises across Europe.

The chart shows adoption by enterprise size class in 2022. Select a country to compare against the EU27 average.

Larger enterprises show higher robot adoption in the Eurostat data. This supports a structural reading of adoption, but it should not be treated as a pure size effect because sector composition also differs across firm sizes.

Manufacturing leads sector adoption

The heatmap shows the cross-country mean robot adoption for each economic sector in 2018, 2020 and 2022. Manufacturing dominates every year (around 17 to 18%), far above the service-oriented sectors, which mostly sit between 1 and 4%. Select a country on the small map to switch the heatmap to that country’s own sector values.

Manufacturing-heavy sectors show stronger robot adoption patterns than many service sectors. The visualisation frames this as a pattern, not a causal decomposition.

Research collaboration does not strongly predict adoption

The map now shows robotics research collaboration links between the 12-country overlap subset (countries present in both Eurostat and ETO data). Node size reflects hub strength (collaboration articles), while the map shades each country by its robot adoption level.

Germany, France, and Italy are central research hubs, but their enterprise adoption rates vary. Click a country to see its collaboration partners.

The overlap subset does not show strong evidence of a positive association between research hub strength and enterprise robot adoption. This should be read as absence of strong evidence in the available data, not evidence that research collaboration has no influence.

Key takeaways

The chart compares each country’s research hub strength (left) with its enterprise robot adoption (right), sorted by hub strength. Use the year slider to watch the ranking shift across 2018, 2020 and 2022. Countries central in robotics research collaboration are not automatically the ones with the highest enterprise robot adoption.

This supports a mismatch narrative, but does not establish causal separation between research and adoption.
